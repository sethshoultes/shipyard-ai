## featureDream IMPROVE Cycle | 2026-04-30
## Lens: Retention Hooks & What Brings People Back

---

## Executive Summary

**Verdict: We build tools. People remember stories.**

I have spent decades learning what makes an audience stay past the commercial break. It is not production value. It is not cliffhangers alone. It is the feeling that *something is about to happen to someone I care about.*

These products have no "someone." They have users. They have metrics. They have weekly digests and token budgets and status dropdowns. What they do not have is emotion. And emotion is the only retention mechanism that survives when the novelty wears off.

---

## Product-by-Product Retention Analysis

### 1. Shipyard AI (www.shipyard.company)

**The Retention Problem: One-time builds have no sequel.**

A customer pays $2,500-$15,000 for a website. The site launches. The handoff document is delivered. Then... nothing. There is no natural reason to return. The pricing page explicitly says "no subscription, no auto-renew." The maintenance offer is $150/hour, which feels punitive, not inviting.

**What the customer feels:**
- Launch day: Excitement. Pride. "I have a real website."
- Week 2: Nothing. The site works. They forget we exist.
- Month 3: A plugin needs updating. They Google "WordPress help" and find someone cheaper.
- Month 6: We are a receipt in their email. Not a relationship.

**The missing hooks:**
- **Performance storytelling**: "Your site had 1,247 visitors this month. Here is what they did." A monthly narrative of their business, not a server log.
- **Growth milestones**: "You hit 100 contact form submissions. Here is what that means." Celebrate their wins.
- **Content refresh suggestions**: "Spring is coming. Should we update your homepage hero?" Proactive value, not reactive support.
- **Community**: Other Shipyard customers sharing their sites. A "Fleet" page. Pride of belonging.

**Retention score: 2/10.** We are a contractor, not a partner. Contractors get forgotten.

### 2. LocalGenius (localgenius.company)

**The Retention Paradox: The Best Hook Was Never Built.**

The weekly digest is genuinely good. "You answered 47 questions this week. Up 12% from last week." That is a story. That is a reason to open an email.

**But:**
- It is the ONLY reason. One email per week. No daily touchpoints.
- There are no emotional peaks. No milestones. No "You hit 1,000 helpful answers!" badge.
- There is no cliffhanger. The email ends. The user closes it. Nothing makes them think, "I wonder what next week will bring."
- The designed-but-unbuilt features — Pulse notifications, milestone badges, Business Journal — are all retention plays. None shipped.

**What the user feels:**
- Week 1: "This is helpful. I will check my digest on Monday."
- Week 4: "Oh, the digest. I will read it later." (They do not.)
- Week 8: Unsubscribe. Churn. Forgotten.

**The missing hooks:**
- **Pulse**: Daily micro-notifications. "3 new questions waiting." "Your response time is top 10% in your neighborhood." Small dopamine hits between weekly digests.
- **Milestone badges with shareable OG images**: "Maria's Trattoria just answered its 500th question!" The restaurant owner shares this on Instagram. We get organic distribution. They get pride.
- **Weekly cliffhangers**: "Next week, I am testing a new promotion idea for Italian restaurants in Chicago." Anticipation is a drug.
- **Competitive storytelling**: "You moved from #7 to #4 among pizza places in your zip code." Progress, not just data.

**Retention score: 3/10.** The digest is a good first act. There is no second act.

### 3. Dash (WP Command Palette / Team Notes)

**Retention Mechanism: Workflow investment. But shallow.**

The activity log creates a sense of history. Team notes create shared context. But:
- No notifications when someone mentions you. So you do not check back.
- No urgency. Notes sit in "Open" forever. No escalation. No deadlines.
- No insight. "You resolved 12 notes this month" would be a simple but powerful retention hook. Not built.

**What the user feels:** "This is fine. I will use it when I remember." (They do not remember.)

**Retention score: 3/10.** Functional but replaceable by a shared Google Doc.

---

### 4. Pinned (WP Sticky Notes / Agreements)

**Retention Mechanism: Checklist progress. Underutilized.**

The checklist creates mild investment. Users want to check boxes. But:
- No progress dashboard. "You have completed 67% of your agreement checklist" is a retention hook. Not built.
- No deadlines. Agreements without deadlines do not create urgency.
- No sharing. An agreement is usually between two parties. But Pinned has no external sharing mechanism.

**What the user feels:** "I made a checklist. Neat." Then they use DocuSign for the actual agreement.

**Retention score: 2/10.** The seed content creates initial investment, but nothing compounds.

---

### 5. Great Minds Plugin

**Retention Mechanism: Memory. Scoreboard. Retrospectives.**

This is the best-retained product in the portfolio, and it is not even trying to be a consumer product.

**What keeps users coming back:**
- **Memory store**: Past projects, past decisions, past bugs. The system remembers so the user does not have to. That is stickiness.
- **Scoreboard**: Projects completed, tokens saved, debates won. Gamification without being obvious.
- **Retrospectives**: Marcus Aurelius writes post-ship reflections. Users read them. They feel part of a tradition.
- **Bug memory**: "Do not repeat past mistakes." The system learns. The user benefits.

**But:**
- No team features. Single-user only. When that user leaves, the memory is orphaned.
- No public scoreboard. Users cannot compare their agency's output to others. Leaderboards create retention.
- No "streak" mechanic. "You shipped 3 projects in a row." Simple. Effective. Not built.

**Retention score: 6/10.** Best in portfolio because it understands that memory is stickier than features.

## Cross-Portfolio Synthesis

| Product | Retention Score | Core Emotion | Missing Hook |
|---------|----------------|--------------|--------------|
| Shipyard AI | 2/10 | Transactional pride | Ongoing narrative |
| LocalGenius | 3/10 | Curiosity (weekly) | Daily dopamine, milestones |
| Dash | 3/10 | Mild organization | Urgency, insight, notifications |
| Pinned | 2/10 | Checklist satisfaction | Progress dashboards, sharing |
| Great Minds | 6/10 | Mastery, memory | Team memory, streaks, leaderboards |

**Pattern identified**: We track events. We do not craft narratives. A user who sees "47 questions answered" understands a fact. A user who sees "You helped 47 customers this week — more than any other Italian restaurant in your neighborhood" understands a *story*. Stories bring people back. Facts do not.

---

## Recommendations (Ranked by Retention Impact)

### 1. Add Weekly Cliffhangers to LocalGenius Digest — IMMEDIATELY
This is the cheapest, highest-impact retention play in the portfolio. The digest already ships. Add one sentence at the end: "Next week, I am analyzing which responses drive the most walk-in traffic. Stay tuned." Anticipation is free. And it works.

### 2. Build the Pulse Notification System for LocalGenius
Daily micro-touchpoints: "3 new questions waiting." "Your response time beat 89% of competitors yesterday." "A customer bookmarked your answer." These are the emotional peaks between weekly digests. Without them, the user lives in a valley.

### 3. Turn Shipyard AI into a Monthly Storyteller
Every customer gets a "Your Site This Month" email: visitor trends, form submissions, performance score, content suggestions. Not a report. A narrative. "Your homepage hero image loads in 0.8 seconds — that is faster than 73% of restaurant sites we have built." Pride. Progress. Partnership.

### 4. Add Streaks and Leaderboards to Great Minds
"You have shipped 4 projects without a Margaret Hamilton rejection." "Your agency ranks #3 in tokens saved this quarter." These are cheap to build and create powerful social proof and personal investment.

### 5. Give Pinned a Progress Dashboard
Simple bar chart: agreements created, checklists completed, time to resolution. People love watching bars fill. It is psychology so basic it feels like cheating. It is not cheating. It is design.

### 6. Give Dash Urgency
Add due dates. Add "overdue" badges. Add a "This week in Dash" summary email. A note without a deadline is a diary entry. A note with a deadline is a responsibility. Responsibilities bring people back.

---

> **Bottom line**: Tools are rented. Stories are remembered. Every weekly digest that ends without a cliffhanger is a user who forgets to open the next one. Every site launch without a follow-up narrative is a customer who becomes a stranger. We do not need more features. We need more reasons to care.
