# Sara Blakely Review — Homeport (Aftercare)

**Date:** 2026-04-16
**Reviewer:** Sara Blakely (Customer Obsession Lens)

---

## Would a real customer pay for this?

**Maybe.** Not directly — it's retention, not sales. Real value is *preventing churn* and *triggering repeat business*.

Customers don't pay for "nice emails." They pay when you solve a problem *they didn't know they had yet.*

**The hook:** Most agencies ghost after launch. If Homeport catches ONE issue before the customer notices (broken link, security update, expired SSL), it pays for itself 10x. But nothing in this plan proves you're catching issues — just sending nice words.

---

## What's confusing? What would make someone bounce?

**Unsubscribe is too easy.** One-click unsub is legally required, but you're making it *effortless to leave.* Zero friction = zero commitment. Add a "pause for 90 days" option. People want control, not abandonment.

**"Day 7, 30, 90, 180, 365" is engineer thinking, not customer thinking.** Customers don't count days. They think in *events*: "My site launched," "My competitor launched theirs," "I need to update my product line." Your cadence is arbitrary. Why Day 7? Why not "when Google crawls them for the first time"?

**No proof you're watching.** Day 30 email says "we've been watching" — watching *what?* If you're not delivering uptime stats, page speed, or security alerts, it's just spam with a friendly tone.

---

## 30-second elevator pitch (write it)

"Agencies disappear after launch. We don't. Homeport keeps your site alive with friendly check-ins at Day 7, 30, 90, 180, and one year. No upsells. No dashboards. Just a human email that says: 'Your site's still breathing. Need anything?' Most agencies ghost. We stay."

**Pitch grade: B-**
Too product-focused. Customer doesn't care about your 5-email cadence. Lead with the pain: "Your site goes live. Two months later, it's broken. Nobody told you. We fix that."

---

## What would you test first with $0 marketing budget?

**Manual outreach to 3 past customers.**
Pick customers who shipped 7, 30, and 90 days ago. Send the email *by hand* from your personal inbox. No automation. No templates. Just: "Hey [name], your site's been live for [X days]. How's it holding up? Noticed [specific thing]. Want me to look?"

Track replies. If zero responses in 72 hours, your messaging is dead. If you get *one* "Oh wow, I didn't know that was broken" — you have product-market fit.

**Don't automate until you prove humans care.**

---

## What's the retention hook?

**Right now? There isn't one.**

You're sending emails. That's *notification,* not retention. Retention = "I can't leave because I'd lose something valuable."

**Retention hook ideas:**
- Track site uptime and alert *before* customer notices downtime
- Send quarterly "site health report" (load time, mobile score, security scan)
- Offer "1 free fix per year" for customers who stay subscribed
- Build a streak: "Your site has been live for 180 days. We've checked in 4 times. Reply rate: 100%."

**Current plan:** You're betting that *caring* is the hook. That's noble. But caring without *value delivery* is just noise.

---

## Gut Check: Ship or Kill?

**Ship it.** But with guardrails.

You're solving a real problem (post-launch ghosting), but the execution is too passive. You need to *do something,* not just *say something.*

**Before you scale:**
1. Manual test with 5 customers (no automation)
2. Add ONE concrete value delivery (uptime check, SSL expiry alert, Google Search Console snapshot)
3. Measure replies, not opens

If reply rate <10% after 90 days, kill it or pivot to value delivery, not feelings.

---

**Final Word:**
This is a *relationship* play, not a *product* play. Relationships scale poorly. But if you nail the voice and prove you're catching real issues, you'll own customer loyalty forever.

**Go/No-Go: GO** — but watch metrics like a hawk.
