# Relay Case Studies
## Persona: Stephen King (Narrative momentum, vivid everyday detail, pressure builds, dark humor, before/after contrast that feels earned)
## Audience: Prospects evaluating Relay — they need to see themselves in the story and feel the weight of the problem before the relief of the solution.
## Format: Three stories. Each 400–600 words. Real detail. No corporate metrics.

---

## Case Study 1: The Fitness Studio That Almost Drowned in Enthusiasm

**Subject:** Maria Gonzalez, Serenity Fitness Studio, Austin TX

---

The worst day started with an email.

Maria opened Gmail at 6:45 AM, still in her running shoes, still sweating from the 5:30 spin class she'd taught. Seventy-three new messages since last night. She didn't flinch anymore. Seventy was normal. Ninety was a bad day. One-twenty was a Monday.

She sorted them at the front desk while her second class warmed up on the bikes behind her. Most were contact form submissions from the website. Some were real. Most were noise.

*Looking for a gym near me.*
*What are your rates?*  
*Can I freeze my membership?*
*SPECIAL OFFER!!! WE BUY HOUSES!!!*
*Do you have childcare?*
*We can improve your SEO rankings by 400%.*

Maria had a system. It was called "scan, sigh, drag to folder." It took her an hour every morning. Sometimes she missed things. Last Tuesday she found a corporate wellness inquiry from a 200-employee tech company buried under three Viagra ads and a Nigerian prince. She'd replied three days late. They'd already signed with LA Fitness.

She told her husband Alex that night. "I need a receptionist just for email."

"We can't afford one," Alex said. He was right. The studio cleared maybe four grand a month after rent and equipment leases. A receptionist would eat half of that.

That night at 11 PM, Maria sat on their couch with a glass of wine and scrolled through the gym's WordPress dashboard. There was a plugin called Relay. The description said something about AI reading your contact forms and sorting them for you. She almost skipped it. Another AI scam. But the reviews were real. Three hundred of them. Mostly from small business owners. One said: "It's like hiring a twenty-dollar-a-month intern who actually reads your email."

She installed it.

The next morning she didn't open Gmail first. She opened Relay's inbox. Forty-two submissions from the night before. Color-coded. Sales inquiries in green. Support questions in blue. Spam in red, already filtered to a separate tab. There was the corporate wellness lead — marked Sales, High Urgency. She'd missed it by five hours, but now it had her attention.

She clicked one button. Relay routed it to her priority email with the subject line `[SALES • HIGH] Corporate wellness inquiry — 200 employees`.

Maria finished her coffee before her first class.

That was six months ago. She still scours Gmail occasionally, like checking the basement after the plumber fixed the leak. But Relay catches everything now. The Viagra ads go to Spam. The SEO robots go to Spam. The real questions go to her team. The corporate wellness contract closed three weeks ago. Two hundred employees. Twelve-month commitment.

Maria doesn't talk about Relay much. It's just there. Like the spin bikes. Like the AC. Like having hot water.

Some days she forgets how bad it was. Then she runs into a gym owner friend at a conference who complains about unread contact forms, and Maria feels a chill — not fear, exactly, but the memory of fear. Like remembering a basement flood.

She tells them about Relay. She tells them it's twenty bucks a month. She tells them it's like hiring an intern.

She doesn't tell them about the morning she almost gave up.

## Case Study 2: The Agency That Outgrew Their Inbox

**Subject:** Darnell Mitchell, DarnellTech Solutions, Remote/San Diego

---

Darnell ran his agency from a standing desk in his garage. Twelve clients. Forty-seven websites. His team was four people on Slack, three in India, one he'd never met in person.

He didn't take meetings. He didn't do phone calls. He communicated in code comments and pull requests and terse Slack messages at 2 AM. When clients needed to reach him, they filled out contact forms. Those forms went to a Gmail filter called CLIENT-LEADS that Darnell checked on Tuesdays.

Tuesdays weren't enough.

The trouble started with a hotel chain. They'd submitted a form through one of Darnell's agency sites asking for a quote on a forty-property booking platform. Darnell saw it seventeen days later. By then they'd hired a competitor in Denver for $80,000.

He didn't tell anyone about the lost deal. He told his wife it was "bad timing." But he lay awake that night knowing the truth: he was a good developer and a terrible business owner. And the gap between those two things was going to kill the agency.

The next Monday he installed Relay. It took eight minutes. He didn't read the docs. He pasted his Claude API key, added his biz-dev person's email to the Sales routing field, and went back to debugging a WooCommerce checkout error.

An hour later his phone buzzed. It was BizDev Karen. "Hey, I just got an email about a restaurant group looking for reservations software. High urgency. Did you see this?"

"No," Darnell said. "Relay sent it."

"What's Relay?"

"The thing that reads my email so I don't have to."

Karen laughed. She thought he was joking.

Three weeks later Karen closed three new deals. All from forms Darnell would have found in two weeks. All from leads Relay had caught and classified while he slept. The restaurant group signed for $24,000. A dental chain signed for $18,000. A boutique fitness studio — that one made him pause, thinking about Maria — signed for $12,000.

Darnell didn't change. He still checked email on Tuesdays. But now the important stuff didn't wait for Tuesday. It found Karen on Monday morning.

One night in October, at 1:47 AM, Darnell opened Relay's admin panel for no reason except he was curious. Two hundred and thirty-seven leads classified since installation. Sixty-two marked Spam. Forty-one Support requests routed to his account manager. One hundred thirty-four Sales leads.

He stared at the screen for a long moment. Then he closed the tab and went back to his code.

He didn't tell Karen about the numbers. He didn't write a blog post. But sometimes at 2 AM, debugging some client's broken plugin, he felt something unfamiliar.

It wasn't exactly peace of mind. It was the absence of dread.

## Case Study 3: The Hotel That Lost a Wedding — and Then Never Lost Another

**Subject:** Kevin O'Brien, O'Brien's Boutique Hotel, Savannah GA

---

Kevin's hotel had fourteen rooms. It was the kind of place where the concierge remembered your anniversary and the lobby smelled like lemon furniture polish. Kevin ran it with his sister Siobhan. She handled guests. He handled everything else: bookings, billing, the website, the contact form.

The contact form was the problem.

The hotel's site was WordPress with a simple form: Name, Email, Dates, Message. Submissions went to hotel@savannahobriens.com. Kevin checked it twice a day. Morning and night. Between those checks the emails piled up.

Most were noise. Travel blog pitches. SEO consultants. A man in Nigeria who wanted to book all fourteen rooms for a "religious conference" if Kevin would just wire a deposit.

But some were real. And real meant money.

October 14th. Kevin woke up at 6 AM with a sinus headache. He skipped his morning email check and drove to the pharmacy. When he finally opened Gmail at 10:30, there were forty-three new messages. He skimmed them between checking in a guest and replacing a broken toilet handle in Room 7.

He didn't see the inquiry from the Charleston couple until 7 PM.

*We're getting married next June and looking for a boutique hotel for our rehearsal dinner and guest block. Do you host events?*

Kevin replied immediately. But the couple had already booked the Andaz downtown. Twenty-two rooms. Three nights. A rehearsal dinner for eighty people. Lost because Kevin had been plunging a toilet while their email sat unread.

He told Siobhan he was sorry. She said it wasn't his fault. But he heard her on the phone that night with their mother, saying they probably needed to hire someone for the website. They couldn't afford that. The hotel made money in spring and lost it in winter. Hiring help in October was fantasy.

That night Kevin searched "WordPress plugin sorts contact form submissions." He found Relay on page two of the plugin directory. He installed it without reading the description. He was too tired to be skeptical.

The next morning he woke to a different kind of inbox. Relay had processed forty overnight submissions. Three were marked Sales, High Urgency. Two were marked Sales, Medium. The rest — SEO pitches, Viagra, the Nigerian conference — were red badges in the Spam tab.

One of the High Urgency leads was a corporate retreat from a law firm. Twelve rooms, four nights, March dates. Kevin replied at 6:15 AM, before his coffee was done. They booked by noon.

Siobhan didn't understand how he found it so fast. "Did you start waking up earlier?"

"No," Kevin said. "I got a filter."

"Like Gmail?"

"Smarter than Gmail."

She didn't ask more. Siobhan trusted technology the way she trusted airplanes: she didn't need to understand the engine to believe it would stay in the air.

The hotel didn't change overnight. They still had leaky toilets and slow winters and guests who complained about the ice machine. But they didn't lose any more leads to bad timing. The Charleston inquiry still haunted Kevin — the what-if of that afternoon. But it was a ghost now, fading. Like remembering a fever dream.

In April, Relay flagged another wedding inquiry. Savannah couple. June booking. Kevin saw it at 6:04 AM, replied at 6:05. They booked the guest block by lunch.

That night Kevin and Siobhan opened a bottle of champagne. Not the cheap stuff they served at the complimentary guest happy hour. The real thing.

"To filters," Siobhan said.

"To AI that reads my email," Kevin corrected.

They drank. The hotel was quiet. Fourteen rooms, all booked for the weekend. The ice machine hummed down the hall. Everything was working.

Even the contact form.

## Voice Notes (Stephen King)

- **Open with a problem in motion.** Don't describe the business; show the moment it breaks.
- **Specific details win.** Running shoes, 5:30 spin class, lemon furniture polish, broken toilet handle. These make the story feel remembered, not invented.
- **The solution arrives quietly.** No fanfare. No transformation montage. Just install, configure, move on.
- **The impact is emotional, not numerical.** Not "47% more leads closed" — it's finishing coffee before class, or the absence of dread, or champagne in the quiet hotel.
- **Dark threads.** The lost deals don't vanish. They become ghosts. The relief is real but earned.
- **Every story has a physical setting you can smell.** The garage agency. The fitness studio lobby. The Savannah hotel hallway.
