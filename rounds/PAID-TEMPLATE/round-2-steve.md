# Steve Jobs — Round 2 Rebuttal

Elon, you want to optimize tokens per project. I want to optimize *courage per customer.*
Your "rigid scope guardrails" turn human ambition into an assembly line.
That is how you get SaaS mediocrity—fast, scalable, and utterly forgettable.
You are optimizing for engineering throughput when you should be optimizing for the moment
a customer sees their name on a live URL and feels, for the first time, like a founder
instead of a supplicant.

You would attack my "one beautiful question" as romantic and unserious.
You will say: *"Steve, you cannot ship without scope. You cannot scope without checkboxes."*
But the opposite of simplicity is not sophistication; it is clutter.
A confessional is not slow. A priest does not hand you a forty-field survey.
He asks the one question that unlocks everything else.
The data you think you need upfront? You do not need it. You infer it from the build.
The form is a dialogue, not a database dump. We are not building a CRM; we are midwifing a vision.

If the PRD must stay under fifty lines to fit the context window, fine.
The *customer* never sees line fifty-one. We abstract and curate; the constraint belongs to the machine, not the human.

Where you are absolutely right: commercial frontmatter in the PRD is administrative sin.
Strip `stripe_payment_id`, strip the status log, strip anything that smells of Jira.
Git history exists; use it. The build document must be pure.
And yes, at 100x scale, local asset folders die. I concede the CDN.
I concede sharded agents—Design, Component, Deploy—because a 200-line PRD plus a codebase
will not fit in one session. Infrastructure is not where we compete; it is where we comply.
Move the money and legal plumbing to Stripe and HubSpot where it belongs.
I will not romanticize what should be invisible.

I also concede that e-commerce, auth, and multi-language are v2.
You are correct. Each is a 10x complexity multiplier, and v1 must ship.
But when we do ship them, they will not be checkboxes—they will be products with their own confessional and craft.

You say I am a perfectionist. I say you are building a cemetery of "good enough."
Good enough is not a strategy; it is a surrender. The customer knows—they always know.

But you are catastrophically wrong about templates.
A "fixed component library" makes every site identical. Templates are graves.
We are not McDonald's. The customer must feel that what shipped is *theirs* and no one else's.
If that costs more tokens, we buy more tokens. If that demands human taste, we hire taste.
You optimize for the machine; I optimize for the memory the customer carries for ten years.
That is the only metric that builds a brand worth owning.

Your referral loop is mechanically clever, but a "Built by Shipyard" badge is not distribution—
it is graffiti unless it is earned. Make it an invisible signature, not a billboard; let the work speak, then whisper our name.

## My Non-Negotiables

1. **The intake is a conversation, never a form.** Five questions, maximum.
   The customer must feel understood, not processed.

2. **The output is undeniably beautiful.** No rigid component library that breeds sameness.
   We ship craft, not templates.

3. **Our voice is the master craftsman.** Warm, exact, authoritative.
   No buzzwords, no exclamation points, no exceptions.

Everything else is a variable. These three are the product.
