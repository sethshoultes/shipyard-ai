# FormForge: Design & Brand Positions

## Product Naming

**FormForge** is wrong. It's two syllables too many, and it sounds like enterprise software from 2008.

The name should be **Forge**.

One word. A verb. A place where raw materials become something powerful. When you forge something, you create it with heat and intention. That's what a form does — it takes raw user input and shapes it into structured data. *Forge* is memorable, active, and carries the weight of craftsmanship.

## Design Philosophy

The form builder space is a graveyard of drag-and-drop complexity. Typeform made forms beautiful but bloated. Google Forms made them free but forgettable. Gravity Forms made them powerful but impenetrable.

**Forge is the anti-dashboard.**

Every interaction should feel like writing, not configuring. No thirty-tab settings panels. No "advanced mode." The form IS the interface. You see what your users will see, always. The editing experience and the user experience should be indistinguishable — like writing a letter, not programming a machine.

## First 30 Seconds

You click "New Form." You see a blank canvas with a single blinking cursor and the words: *"Ask something."*

You type: "What's your name?"

A name field appears. Elegant. Automatic. No field type dropdown. No validation settings modal. It just *knows*.

That's the moment. That's when the user thinks: "This is different." The first 30 seconds should feel like magic, not homework.

## Brand Voice

Forge speaks like a confident craftsman — not a salesperson, not a tech bro.

Short sentences. Active voice. No exclamation points. We don't say "Amazing!" We say "Done." We don't say "Your form has been successfully created!" We say "Form ready."

The product earns enthusiasm through clarity, not through telling users how excited they should be.

## What to Say NO To

- **No conditional logic on day one.** Ship the 80% use case flawlessly before touching branching.
- **No theme customization panel.** One beautiful theme. If people need brand colors, expose exactly two fields: primary color and logo. That's it.
- **No integrations menu.** If we can't make the integration invisible (email just works, storage just works), we're not ready to ship it.
- **No analytics dashboard.** A form builder builds forms. Counting submissions is a spreadsheet's job.

Complexity is a tax on users. Every feature we add should *remove* a decision, not create one.

## The Emotional Hook

People will love Forge because **it makes them feel competent, not capable.**

Capable means "I learned how to use this tool." Competent means "I'm good at this." The difference is identity. When someone builds a form in Forge and it takes 90 seconds instead of 15 minutes, they don't think "Wow, great software." They think "I'm fast at this."

That's the hook. We make users feel like craftsmen, not operators.

*The best tools disappear. Forge should feel like thinking directly onto the screen.*
