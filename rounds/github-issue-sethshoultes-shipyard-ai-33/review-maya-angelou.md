# Copy Review — Maya Angelou Perspective

*"Words mean more than what is set down on paper. It takes the human voice to infuse them with deeper meaning."*

---

## Overall Impression

This is a form builder called Forge, and I've walked through its soul — its messages, its placeholders, its confirmations, the small moments where it speaks to the people using it.

Here is what I found: **competence without warmth**. The words do their job. They don't stumble. But they don't sing either.

---

## Does the Language Feel Human, Not Corporate?

**Mostly yes, with exceptions.**

The copy avoids the worst corporate sins — no "leverage," no "synergize," no "optimize your workflow." That's good. But it settles into something almost as dangerous: *safe neutrality*.

**The good:**
- "Thank you! We'll get back to you soon." — This breathes. It has rhythm.
- "How can we help you?" — A genuine question, not a command.

**The concerning:**
- "No forms yet. Create your first form to get started!" — This is instructing, not inviting.
- "Email capability not available. Ensure an email provider plugin is installed and configured." — This speaks like a manual, not a friend.
- "This action cannot be undone." — True, necessary, but cold as winter stone.

---

## Is There Rhythm in the Sentences?

**Sometimes.**

When you read "Thank you! We'll get back to you soon," you can feel the pause between gratitude and promise. That's rhythm.

But then: "High confidence - this field type is a strong match." No music there. Just information marching in single file.

The best copy has *breath* — places where the reader can pause, where meaning lands before the next phrase begins. Much of this copy runs straight through like a commuter catching a train.

---

## Does the Headline Stop You?

**No.**

"Forms" is not a headline. It is a label.
"Submissions" is not a headline. It is a filing cabinet.
"Create New Form" tells you what to do, not why you'd want to.

A headline should make you lean in. These make you nod and scroll.

---

## Is Anything Trying Too Hard?

**The opposite problem.**

Nothing here is trying too hard. That's actually the concern. The copy is so careful not to offend, not to presume, not to take up space — that it forgets to *be* anything.

The one place I sense effort: "High confidence - this field type is a strong match." This is explaining itself when it could simply be confident. It's like someone saying "I'm being really sincere right now" — the declaration undermines the truth.

---

## The Three Weakest Lines (and What They Could Become)

### 1. Original:
> "No forms yet. Create your first form to get started!"

**Why it's weak:** It announces emptiness, then gives orders with false enthusiasm. The exclamation point is doing work the words didn't earn.

**Rewritten:**
> "This is where your forms will live. Ready to build your first one?"

*This acknowledges the blank space without apologizing for it. It invites rather than instructs.*

---

### 2. Original:
> "Low confidence - please review and select the appropriate field type"

**Why it's weak:** It confesses uncertainty, uses technical language ("field type"), and puts the burden on the user with "please review." It sounds like a system covering its liability.

**Rewritten:**
> "I made my best guess here. Does this look right to you?"

*This is honest without being clinical. It asks for collaboration, not compliance.*

---

### 3. Original:
> "Email capability not available. Ensure an email provider plugin is installed and configured."

**Why it's weak:** This is a wall of technical words that tells you what's wrong without telling you what it means for *you*. It speaks system, not human.

**Rewritten:**
> "We can't send notification emails yet. Once you add an email provider, every new submission can land right in your inbox."

*This explains the absence, then paints the possibility. It turns a limitation into a doorway.*

---

## A Final Word

Copy is not decoration. It is the voice of the thing.

Every placeholder, every button, every error message is a chance to make someone feel *seen* — or to make them feel like they're negotiating with a machine.

Forge does its work adequately. But adequacy is not what people remember.

They remember how you made them feel.
