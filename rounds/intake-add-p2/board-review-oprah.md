# Board Review: Intake (Add p2 Support)

**Reviewer:** Oprah Winfrey — Board Member, Great Minds Agency
**Perspective:** Human Impact, Trust, and Accessibility

---

## First-5-Minutes Experience: Would a New User Feel Welcomed or Overwhelmed?

Here's what I love: **there is no first-5-minutes experience.** And that's not a failure—that's *wisdom.*

Steve called it "the absence of an experience," and he's exactly right. The best technology doesn't ask for your attention. It doesn't demand you learn a new interface, configure settings, or read documentation. It just... works for you while you sleep.

A developer labels an issue `p2` at 11pm, closes their laptop, and wakes up to a PRD waiting for them. That's not just good UX—that's *respect.* It says: "Your time matters. Your work matters. We'll handle the boring parts."

**Verdict:** Neither welcomed nor overwhelmed—simply *served.* That's actually the highest form of welcome. You're telling people: "We see you. We've got this."

---

## Emotional Resonance: Does This Make People *Feel* Something?

Oh, honey. This is where it gets real.

The essence document says it perfectly: *"Trust. The quiet relief of knowing the system sees you."*

Let me tell you about the emotional wound this heals. Every person who's ever filed a ticket, submitted a request, or raised a concern that disappeared into the void—they carry a scar. It's the feeling of being invisible. Of your work not mattering. Of screaming into the organizational abyss and hearing nothing back.

That sting Steve described—"Wait, I filed that issue three weeks ago. Why hasn't anything happened?"—that's not just frustration. That's a **betrayal of trust.** And every time it happens, people pull back. They stop contributing. They stop believing the system cares about them.

Intake says something radical: **Your medium-priority work matters too.**

Not everything needs to be a fire drill to deserve attention. P2 issues are the daily bread of development—the improvements, the cleanups, the "it would be nice if" ideas. When those get processed automatically, you're telling people: "We value your whole self, not just your emergencies."

**Verdict:** This absolutely makes people feel something—it makes them feel *seen.* And being seen is the foundation of every meaningful relationship, including the one between a person and their tools.

## Trust: Would I Recommend This to My Audience?

Let me reframe this question: Would I recommend the *philosophy* behind this to the people I care about?

**Yes.** A thousand times yes.

Here's why: This isn't just about polling GitHub issues. This is about building systems that honor human intention. When someone takes the time to label something with a priority, they're making a declaration: "This matters to me." A system that ignores that declaration is fundamentally disrespectful.

What I'd tell my audience:

> "Look at how your tools treat your requests. Do they make you chase them? Do they make you wonder if anyone heard you? Or do they simply act—quietly, reliably, without drama? The second kind of tool is the one that respects your time and your energy. Demand systems that see you."

The "no dashboards" decision is particularly wise. A dashboard says "check on us—we might have failed." Intake says "go live your life—we've got this." That's the energy I want people surrounding themselves with.

**One caveat:** I'd want to know how this handles edge cases. What if the p2 issue is unclear? What if the automation misinterprets something? The invisible system needs a visible escape hatch—not a dashboard, but maybe a simple acknowledgment: "Issue #35 converted to PRD. Questions? Reply to this comment." Give people a thread to pull if they need it.

**Verdict:** I would recommend this to anyone building automation. Not the code—the *mindset.* Build things that disappear into service.

## Accessibility: Who's Being Left Out?

Now let's talk about the harder truth. Every system that "just works" is only invisible to those for whom it was designed.

**Who benefits from Intake:**
- Developers who know to use GitHub labels
- Teams with established triage processes
- People comfortable with command-line workflows
- Those who trust automated systems

**Who might be left out:**

1. **The label-hesitant:** Some people don't label issues because they don't feel confident their judgment of priority is "correct." They need permission and guidance, not just silent automation.

2. **The non-technical stakeholders:** Product managers, designers, and customers who file issues but don't speak the p0/p1/p2 language. If labeling is required for attention, they're invisible to Intake by design.

3. **The observability-needers:** Some people *need* the dashboard. Not because they expect failure, but because visibility creates confidence. "I can't see it working, so I don't trust it's working." Steve would say that's a personal growth opportunity. I'd say it's a legitimate cognitive style we shouldn't dismiss.

4. **International and non-English speakers:** The logs, error messages, and documentation all assume English fluency. Not a v1 problem, but worth noting.

**What I'd ask the team:**

> "Invisible infrastructure is beautiful when everyone has equal access to make themselves visible. What happens to the issue someone files *without* a priority label? Does Intake's existence create a shadow system where unlabeled work is silently deprioritized?"

**Verdict:** The accessibility gaps here are about *access to the on-ramp,* not the system itself. Ensure the path to "get your work seen" is clear to everyone, not just label-fluent developers.

---

## Final Score: 8/10

**One-line justification:** Intake embodies the sacred principle that systems should serve people invisibly—but needs to ensure the path to being seen is equally invisible to *everyone,* not just those who already know the secret handshake.

## The Oprah Take

Here's what I want everyone on this board to understand:

The decisions document says the essence of Intake is: *"What is this product REALLY about? Making work matter by ensuring nothing falls through the cracks."*

That's not a product statement. That's a **moral commitment.**

Every time we build automation, we're making a choice about whose labor we value and whose we overlook. Intake chooses to value the middle—the p2 work that's not urgent but still important. That's radical in a world that only responds to emergencies.

Steve and Elon debated dashboards vs. logs, hardcoding vs. config, shipping today vs. optimizing for scale. All good discussions. But the real question is: **Does this help people feel that their contributions matter?**

The answer is yes. Ship it.

But also: Never stop asking who we haven't invited to the table yet. Invisible systems are only equitable when everyone can make themselves visible through them.
