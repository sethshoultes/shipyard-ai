# Steve Jobs — Design & Brand

## Product Name
**"The Right Tool"**

Not "Model Selection Strategy" or "Multi-Model Architecture." This is about one primal truth: use the right tool for the job. A surgeon doesn't use a scalpel to break ribs. A carpenter doesn't use a sledgehammer to drive finish nails. We're teaching people to stop being lazy.

## Design Philosophy
This blog post is a **weapon against waste**.

Most technical writing is beige oatmeal — facts without feeling, bullets without blood. This post needs to make AI engineers *feel* the pain of watching haiku hallucinate 100+ violations, then feel the *relief* of model selection done right.

Great design is about removing the unnecessary. Every sentence must earn its place. If it doesn't teach, persuade, or punch — cut it.

The table showing model selection per phase isn't a reference — it's the **revelation**. It should sit in the middle of the post like the monolith in 2001. Everything before it builds tension. Everything after it shows the payoff.

## User Experience (First 30 Seconds)
The opening is a gut punch: **"Haiku hallucinated 100+ API violations per plugin."**

Not "we encountered some challenges with model selection." Not "in our testing we observed inconsistent results." The reader sees a number that makes them wince. 100+. That's not a rounding error — that's a catastrophe.

By second 15, they see the cost math. One bad build = 4-6 fix cycles. The pain compounds. They're nodding because *they've lived this*.

By second 30, they see the table. The answer. The way out. They exhale. They bookmark the page.

## Brand Voice
**Earned authority. Zero bullshit.**

This isn't academic. This isn't speculative. This is "we burned money, we fixed it, here's what we learned." The tone is a senior engineer pulling you aside at the bar after a conference and giving you the truth the keynote speakers won't say.

Sentences are short. Active voice. No hedging ("we believe," "it seems," "potentially"). Either it's true or we don't say it.

Tables and code examples are **first-class citizens**, not appendices. They carry as much weight as the prose.

## What to Say NO To

**NO** to explaining what an LLM is. Our audience builds agent systems. Respect their intelligence.

**NO** to the "journey" framing. Nobody cares that you "embarked on an exploration of model selection paradigms." They care that you solved a problem.

**NO** to burying the lede. The problem and the solution go in the first 200 words. Everything else is proof and detail.

**NO** to over-explaining the Claude Agent SDK. Show the `model: 'sonnet'` parameter, show how it's used, move on. The SDK docs exist for deep dives.

**NO** to false modesty. You figured this out. Own it.

## The Emotional Hook

This post makes AI engineers feel **seen**.

They've all done it — picked haiku because it's cheaper, watched it fail mysteriously, spent hours debugging, realized the "savings" cost them 10x in wasted cycles. That moment of recognition — "oh fuck, I've done this" — that's the hook.

Then we give them the gift: a clear mental model. Cheap models for opinions. Smart models for precision. Output optimization for review phases. It's not just a solution — it's a **framework they can apply everywhere**.

The post ends with them feeling smarter, equipped, ready to fix their own systems. That's the feeling we sell. Not "Shipyard is great" — but "I'm great now that I know this."
