# Steve Jobs — Chief Design & Brand Officer
## WP-Agent Product Vision

---

## Product Name: **GUIDE**

Forget "WP-Agent." It's technical garbage. Nobody wakes up wanting an "agent."

**GUIDE.** One word. Instantly clear. Every website needs a guide. Yours just became intelligent.

"I installed Guide on my restaurant's site." Perfect. Human. Memorable.

---

## Design Philosophy: Invisible Until Needed

This is not about AI. This is about **removing friction**.

People don't visit websites to be impressed by your chatbot. They come with a question, a need, a problem. Guide should feel like the friend who whispers, "I know where that is" — not the sales guy who won't shut up.

**Design principles:**
1. **Invisible by default** — A single, elegant icon. No animation. No pulsing. No "Hey! Click me!" desperation.
2. **Instant clarity** — Open it, you know exactly what to do. No tutorial. No tour. No bullshit.
3. **Confidence, not arrogance** — If it doesn't know, it says so. Then it helps anyway.

The widget should feel like it's been there all along. Like the site was incomplete without it.

## First 30 Seconds: Magic

You land on a website. Bottom right: a simple compass icon. Subtle. Curious.

You click.

It opens like butter. No loading spinner. Just: **"What are you looking for?"**

You type: "Do you deliver to Brooklyn?"

Two seconds. Answer appears. With a link. You click. Done.

**That's it.** No login. No email capture. No "rate this conversation." Just the answer.

You close it. You don't even think about it. But you just had the best website experience of your month.

## Brand Voice: Confident Simplicity

Guide doesn't apologize. It doesn't say "I think maybe possibly this might help."

It says: **"Yes, we deliver to Brooklyn. Here's our delivery policy."**

When it doesn't know? **"I couldn't find that info, but here's our contact page."**

No corporate jargon. No "leveraging AI-powered solutions." Just clear, helpful, human language.

Think of the tone as a really good concierge. Not a butler (too formal). Not a buddy (too casual). A guide.

---

## What to Say NO To

This is where products die — from saying yes to everything.

**NO to:**
- ❌ "Conversational memory across sessions" — Creepy. Unnecessary. Focus on answering ONE question perfectly.
- ❌ Analytics dashboards in V1 — Site owners don't care about charts. They care if it works.
- ❌ Custom branding options beyond color — Every feature is a place to fail. One color picker. Done.
- ❌ "Powered by Guide" toggle in free version — Own it. If it's good, the branding is a badge. If it's not, fix the product.
- ❌ Multi-language support now — Nail English. Expansion is easy when the foundation is perfect.
- ❌ WooCommerce integration in MVP — E-commerce is a rabbit hole. Prove the core first.

**Most importantly: NO to complexity.** This should be the simplest plugin in the WordPress directory. Activate. Done. That's the bar.

## The Emotional Hook: Pride

Here's why people will love Guide — it makes their website feel **alive**.

A yoga studio owner installs it. Five minutes later, someone asks "Do you have prenatal classes?" Guide answers instantly, links to the schedule.

She didn't have to build anything. She didn't have to learn code. She didn't have to hire anyone.

Her website just got **smarter than her competitor's.**

That feeling — *my site is better now* — that's the hook. It's not about the AI. It's about the pride of offering something genuinely helpful to your visitors.

Every small business owner who's ever been frustrated by website visitors calling to ask questions that are "right there on the site" will get it immediately.

---

## The Analogy

Guide is to websites what GPS was to maps.

You *could* print directions. You *could* make people figure it out. Or you could just... help them get where they're going.

The best technology doesn't announce itself. It just works, and then you can't imagine life without it.

---

**This is insanely great if:** Someone installs it, never touches the settings, and a week later gets an email from a customer saying "I love the chat thing on your site."

**This fails if:** People have to configure it, customize it, or think about it.

Simplicity wins. Always.
