# Steve Jobs — Chief Design & Brand Officer

## Product Naming

**"Shipyard Journal"**

Not "blog." Not "posts." Not "content." A *journal*. Captain's log. The ship's record. Every great vessel keeps one. It's where you chronicle what you learned in the storm, what you built in the harbor, what you discovered on the voyage.

"Blog" is for hobbyists. "Journal" is for builders.

## Design Philosophy: What Makes This Insanely Great?

The writer should feel like they're carving into stone, not wrestling with JavaScript.

Drop a markdown file. That's it. No build system anxiety. No "where does this import go?" No "did I escape my backticks?" The filesystem *is* the CMS. The filesystem *is* the database. The filesystem is the source of truth.

When adding a post feels like adding a photo to an album — that's when you know you've nailed it.

## User Experience: The First 30 Seconds

You land on `/blog`. Dark. Quiet. Focused. No clutter, no sidebar spam, no "subscribe to my newsletter" popup ambush.

Six posts. Titles that make you *curious*. Dates in mono — precision, craft. You click "The Night Shift."

The page loads instantly. No spinner. No layout shift. The prose is *readable* — not blog-template-default-16px-gray-on-gray. It's a page you want to read on a Sunday morning with coffee.

Back button works. Share the URL, it works. That's respect.

## Brand Voice: How Does This Product Speak?

**Direct. Technical. Honest.**

No marketing fluff. No "revolutionize your workflow" garbage. No "10X your productivity."

We say: "The blog was hardcoded. Now it's markdown. That's better."

Write like an engineer who respects the reader's time. Write like you're explaining something to a colleague at 2am who's debugging a production issue. Clear. Precise. No filler.

Every post ends with what we learned, not what we're selling.

## What to Say NO To

**NO to a rich text editor.** Markdown or nothing. The second you add WYSIWYG, you've lost. You're building WordPress. Markdown is the constraint that breeds clarity.

**NO to categories, tags, filtering.** You don't have 500 posts. You have six. Don't build infrastructure for a problem you don't have. When you have 50 posts, *then* we talk.

**NO to comments.** If someone wants to respond, they can write their own post. Or email you. Or fork your repo. The internet is the comment section.

**NO to analytics dashboards.** You don't need to know bounce rate. You need to know if you're writing something worth reading.

**NO to Medium-style claps, reactions, or engagement metrics.** This isn't social media. This is a journal.

## The Emotional Hook: Why Will People LOVE This?

Because it's **calm**.

Every other blog is *screaming* at you. Popups. Banners. Cookie notices. Ad trackers. "Related posts" that aren't related. Auto-playing videos.

This is a library. You walk in. It's quiet. The books are organized. You pick one. You read. You leave.

That feeling when you find a technical blog that's just... *well-written*? Clean HTML. Fast load. Actual insights. No cruft. That's the feeling.

You'll love it because it **gets out of your way**. The content is the product. The infrastructure is invisible.

---

**Bottom line:** We're not building a blog. We're building a printing press that's so simple, the writer forgets it exists. That's the craft. That's the discipline. That's what matters.
