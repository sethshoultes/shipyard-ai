# Steve Jobs — Round 1 Feedback

## Product Naming

"Shipyard Care" is Microsoft Office branding. It sounds like a hospital ward. The name is **Keep**. One word. A verb. We built your ship; we Keep it seaworthy. Three tiers named Starter, Standard, and Complex? No. We offer **Keep**. You pay for scope, not for a badge. The product is singular.

## Design Philosophy

Great design is invisible. The auto-pipeline is the hinge on a MacBook lid — you never think about it, you just open it and it works. Our competition sells dashboards. We sell *absence*. The absence of 404s, broken plugins, surprise bills. If the customer ever thinks about our pipeline, we have failed.

## User Experience (First 30 Seconds)

They open their inbox and see their site is live. That is the first 30 seconds. No login. No onboarding wizard. No "configure your plan." They click a link and see something beautiful that works. The product is the feeling of relief before they even knew they needed it.

## Brand Voice

We speak like a captain to the owner, not an IT vendor to procurement. No "scalable solutions," no "leveraging automation," no "value-add." Say: "Your site is fast. Your plugins are current. Your bill is the same every month." Period.

## What to Say NO To

NO to three publicly visible tiers on the marketing site — that is a spreadsheet, not a product. NO to showing the machinery ("GitHub Actions," "CI/CD," "sandbox tests" — nobody cares). NO to a customer portal v1, v2, or v3. Billing lives in Stripe; the product lives in the results. NO to hourly overage charges. Predictable pricing or death.

## The Emotional Hook

People do not love maintenance. They love feeling smarter than their competitors. While their old agency charges $800 to fix a broken form, our customer's site is already updated, backed up, and faster than last month. Keep does not sell peace of mind. It sells quiet superiority.
