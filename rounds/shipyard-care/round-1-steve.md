# Steve Jobs — Round 1: Shipyard Care

## Product Naming

Kill "Shipyard Care." It sounds like a nursing home. You don't want customers feeling cared *for*—you want them feeling *powerful*.

**Call it: Pulse.**

"Shipyard Pulse." One word. It's alive. It's the heartbeat of their website. Every month, you're not sending them a "report"—you're showing them their site's *vital signs*. When someone asks "How's your website doing?" they should say "Let me check the Pulse."

---

## Design Philosophy

Everyone's building dashboards that look like Excel spreadsheets mated with a Bloomberg terminal. Stop. This isn't for data analysts—it's for business owners who have *three minutes* between meetings.

The philosophy: **One number that matters, everything else fades away.**

When you open Pulse, you see ONE metric dominating the screen—their "Site Health Score." Big. Beautiful. Confident. It's like stepping on a scale that also tells you you're doing great. The details exist, but they *whisper*. Progressive disclosure isn't a design pattern—it's respect for your customer's time.

---

## User Experience: The First 30 Seconds

The email lands. Subject line: "Your site had a great month." Not "Monthly Performance Report #47."

They open it. Three seconds to the dopamine hit: their visitor count, up 34%, rendered in a gorgeous single card. No scrolling required.

They click to the dashboard. It loads instantly—under one second or we've failed. The health score pulses gently (literally animates like a heartbeat). Green means go. They *feel* good before they've read a single word.

Thirty seconds. They know their site is alive, healthy, and improving. They close the tab feeling like a *genius* for hiring Shipyard.

---

## Brand Voice

Not corporate. Not cute. **Confident and warm.**

Think of how a great doctor talks: "Your site is performing beautifully. Here's one thing that could make it even stronger." Not "ACTION REQUIRED: 7 CRITICAL ISSUES DETECTED."

Never apologize. Never use exclamation points. Never say "just" or "simply." Speak like someone who knows what they're doing and genuinely wants the customer to win.

The monthly email should feel like a note from a trusted advisor, not a SaaS notification blast.

---

## What to Say NO To

**Kill the ticket system.** You're building a relationship product, not a help desk. The moment you add tickets, you've admitted the product can't stand on its own. If they need something, they reply to an email like a human being.

**Kill Slack notifications.** Nobody needs more Slack noise. If something is truly urgent (site down), send a text or call them. If it's not urgent, it can wait for the monthly Pulse.

**Kill the competitor monitoring report.** You're not a spy agency. Stay focused on making *their* site great, not obsessing over others.

**Kill dark mode.** Ship one thing. Ship it perfectly. Dark mode is a week of engineering for 12% of users who'll toggle it once and forget.

---

## The Emotional Hook

Here's the truth about website owners: **they're terrified.**

They paid thousands for a site and then... silence. Is it working? Is it broken? Is anyone even visiting? They refresh Google Analytics once a month, see numbers they don't understand, and close the tab feeling vaguely anxious.

Pulse is the cure for that anxiety.

Every month, they get *proof* their investment is working. Not just data—*narrative*. "Your site outperformed 85% of businesses in your industry." That sentence is worth $99/month. That's the sentence they forward to their business partner. That's the sentence that makes them feel smart.

We're not selling maintenance. We're selling **peace of mind and permission to stop worrying.**

When someone cancels Pulse, they should feel the anxiety creep back. Not because we've manipulated them—because we've genuinely been protecting them, and now they're alone again.

That's the hook. That's why they'll love it. That's why they'll stay.
