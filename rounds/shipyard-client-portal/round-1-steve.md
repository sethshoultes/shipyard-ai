# Steve Jobs: Design & Brand Vision
**Product:** Shipyard Client Portal
**Date:** 2026-04-15

---

## Product Name: **Shipyard**

Not "Shipyard Portal" or "Shipyard Client Dashboard." Just **Shipyard**. Like iTunes wasn't "iTunes Music Store Manager." The product IS Shipyard. The public site is marketing. This is the product.

---

## Design Philosophy: Calm Power

This isn't a dashboard. It's a **mission control center** for people who build things that matter.

Every pixel must whisper: "We've got this. You can focus on your business."

Think of the first iPhone. When you slid to unlock, you didn't see a mess of widgets and notifications. You saw **clarity**. That's what clients see when they log into Shipyard: their projects, their status, their next move. Nothing else.

**The design rule:** If it doesn't help the client understand "where am I?" or "what happens next?", delete it.

No bloated sidebars. No overwhelming option paralysis. No enterprise software ugliness.

---

## The First 30 Seconds

A client logs in. They see **one thing**: their project, alive, moving forward.

Not a grid of cards. Not a table of data. **One active project**, center stage, with a progress ring that actually means something. Status: "BUILD — Wave 2/4." Estimated completion: "2 days."

Below it? A single sentence: "Your staging site will be ready for review tomorrow."

That's it. That's the entire screen.

If they have multiple projects, they're in a sidebar—minimal, secondary. The hero is always the active project. The one that matters right now.

**Emotion:** Relief. Confidence. "These people know what they're doing."

---

## Brand Voice: Direct Truth

No corporate speak. No "We're excited to inform you that your deliverable has transitioned to the staging environment."

Instead: "Your site is ready. Take a look."

No hedging. No passive voice. No buzzwords.

When something breaks: "The build failed. We're fixing it. You'll get an update in 2 hours."

Not: "We're currently experiencing technical difficulties and will provide a status update as soon as more information becomes available."

**Voice principle:** Write like you're texting a friend who hired you to build something important. Respectful, but human.

---

## What to Say NO To

### 1. **Real-time chat**
Already out of scope. Good. Chat creates expectation of instant response. Email and Slack are enough. Don't build a support burden into the product.

### 2. **Multi-user team accounts**
Not yet. Complexity killer. One client, one login. When we have 100 paying customers, then we'll think about teams.

### 3. **Customization**
No theme pickers. No "choose your accent color." One design. Everyone gets the same experience. This isn't WordPress.

### 4. **Overwhelming analytics**
The PRD lists page views, conversion tracking, Core Web Vitals, load time, uptime monitoring... STOP.

Show three numbers:
- **Visitors this week**
- **Key conversions** (form submissions, whatever matters)
- **Site status** (green dot = up, red dot = down)

That's it. If they want Google Analytics, they can install it themselves.

### 5. **Token budget displays**
"185K tokens remaining" means nothing to a client. They don't care about tokens. Show them: "3 content updates remaining this month." Or better: "Update anything. We'll let you know if you're running low."

Don't make them do math.

## The Emotional Hook

### Why will people LOVE this?

Because it's the opposite of every SaaS dashboard they've ever used.

Every other tool screams: "LOOK AT ALL THESE FEATURES! CONFIGURE ME! LEARN MY COMPLEXITY!"

Shipyard whispers: "Your website is being built. Here's where it is. Here's what's next. Go run your business."

**The hook is peace of mind.**

You ever use Stripe? You log in, and it just... works. You see your revenue, your customers, your next payout. No friction. No confusion.

That's what Shipyard feels like. Clients will love it because **they won't have to think about it**.

---

## The Moment That Matters

It's not the intake form. It's not the payment screen.

It's the moment, three days after they pay, when they log back in and see:

**"Your site is live."**
**[View Site →]**

And they click, and it's *beautiful*. It's *theirs*. It works.

That's the moment they tell their friends.

That's the moment they subscribe to the retainer.

That's the moment Shipyard stops being a vendor and becomes **the team they trust**.

---

## Final Thought

This PRD is 90% right. It understands the problem. It knows the business model.

But it's thinking like an engineer, not a designer.

**The opportunity:** Build the first AI website service that feels like working with a world-class agency—but without the meetings, the confusion, the waiting.

Make clients feel *taken care of*. Not like they're managing a vendor.

That's insanely great.
