# PromptOps — Steve Jobs Position Paper

## Product Naming

Kill "PromptOps." It's a mouthful. It sounds like IT middleware. Nobody falls in love with middleware.

**The name is: Tuned.**

One word. Musical. It implies precision, craft, and something that *works*. "My prompts are Tuned." "I Tuned my agent." It's a verb and a noun. It evokes a finely crafted instrument, not a DevOps dashboard.

Second choice: **Cast** — like casting a spell, or casting actors in a film. Your prompts are performers. You cast them.

We're not selling prompt management. We're selling *prompt mastery*.

## Design Philosophy

This product must feel like a musical instrument, not a control panel.

Every great instrument shares three traits: immediate feedback, progressive mastery, and the ability to make something beautiful in the first session. A guitar rewards you instantly — pluck a string, hear a note. A year later, you're playing jazz. That's our design target.

What makes this *insanely great* is the gap between first use and expert use. A beginner should get magical results in 30 seconds. An expert should discover depth they didn't know existed after 30 days. Same interface. Different skill.

The cardinal sin is the spreadsheet aesthetic. Prompts are not rows in a database. They are living things — they evolve, they have versions, they have moods. The UI must feel alive.

## First 30 Seconds

You open Tuned. No signup wall. No tutorial modal. You see one thing: a prompt you just used, already analyzed.

The interface shows three things immediately:
1. What your prompt *actually* asked (parsed intent)
2. Where it was vague (highlighted uncertainty)
3. One specific improvement (not ten — ONE)

You click the suggestion. It applies. The before/after runs side-by-side. You *see* the difference. Dopamine hits.

You are now hooked. You didn't read documentation. You didn't watch a video. You learned by doing — the only way humans actually learn.

## Brand Voice

Confident but not arrogant. Direct but not cold. The way a great music teacher talks — they tell you what's wrong, show you how to fix it, and make you feel capable.

Never: "Perhaps you might consider..." or "It appears that..."
Always: "This prompt has a problem. Here's the fix."

The voice knows more than you. That's why you're here. But it never makes you feel stupid. It makes you feel like you're becoming someone who knows.

No jargon. "Embedding" becomes "meaning fingerprint." "Tokenization" becomes "how the AI reads this." If a term requires a glossary, it doesn't belong in the interface.

## What to Say NO To

**NO to prompt libraries.** Everyone thinks they want 10,000 templates. Nobody uses them. They want *their* prompts to work better, not someone else's prompts to copy.

**NO to collaboration features in V1.** Teams are V2. Individuals first. Build for the solo practitioner who's frustrated at midnight, not the enterprise buyer who schedules demos.

**NO to analytics dashboards.** Charts are a crutch for products that don't know what to show you. We know. We show the one thing that matters.

**NO to integrations in V1.** No Slack bots. No API keys. No OAuth flows. Copy-paste is the MVP. It works everywhere. Solve the core loop first.

**NO to pricing tiers that create feature anxiety.** One price. Full product. Usage limits are fine. Feature walls are not.

## The Emotional Hook

People will love Tuned because it makes them feel *competent* in a world that makes them feel behind.

Everyone's prompting now. Everyone knows their prompts suck. Everyone sees others getting magical results and wonders "what do they know that I don't?" That gap — between knowing AI is powerful and knowing how to wield it — is emotional. It's embarrassing. It's frustrating.

Tuned closes that gap. Not with a course. Not with a community. With immediate feedback that makes you better *right now*.

The hook isn't "prompt engineering made easy." Easy implies simple, implies maybe not worth learning. The hook is: **"Finally understand what you're saying to AI."**

That's the promise. You've been talking. Now learn to speak.
